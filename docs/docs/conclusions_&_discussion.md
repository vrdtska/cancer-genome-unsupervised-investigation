# Discussion
To contextualize the findings of this pipeline, the results were compared against one of the foundational Glioblastoma molecular study: *"Integrated genomic analysis identifies clinically relevant subtypes of glioblastoma characterized by abnormalities in PDGFRA, IDH1, EGFR, and NF1"* (Verhaak et al., 2010).

## Summary of the Reference Study
Verhaak et al. utilized multidimensional genomic data from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) to classify Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) into distinct molecular subtypes. By applying unsupervised consensus clustering to gene expression data, the authors established four transcriptional subtypes: *Proneural, Neural, Classical, and Mesenchymal*. Their objective was to provide a framework for understanding intratumoral heterogeneity and to guide future targeted therapeutic strategies.

## Methodological Similarities and Differences
- **Similarities**: The core philosophy of both their pipelines and the present study pipelines are similar. Both studies relied on unsupervised clustering methodologies (Hierarchical agglomerative approaches) to partition patient cohorts without prior clinical bias. Furthermore, both pipelines empirically evaluated various values of K using internal validation heuristics, arriving at the similar conclusions: the optimal mathematical and biological division of GBM transcriptomics is K=4 clusters. Both studies also utilized differential expression analysis to extract defining genetic signatures for the resulting clusters. 
- **Differences**: The primary difference lies in the data acquisition technology and feature reduction. Verhaak et al. primarily relied on older gene expression microarray platforms (Affymetrix and Agilent, 2010). In contrast, this project utilized modern, high-resolution bulk RNA-sequencing (STAR-TPM) data, providing a more precise quantification of transcript abundance. Additionally, where Verhaak utilized multi-omics integration (combining RNA with Copy Number Variations and somatic mutations), this pipeline strictly isolated the transcriptomic variance using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) prior to clustering, proving that RNA-level expression alone is somewhat sufficient to drive distinct geometric partitioning.

## Results Comparison and Synthesis
The clinical and biological results of this project align well with the Verhaak study. Verhaak noted massive transcriptomic heterogeneity between the subtypes, defining groups by hyperactive gene expressions —a finding similar to this project's identification of Subtype 0 as a metabolically hyperactive state driven by genes like TFAM and KEAP1. Most importantly, both studies arrived at a  clinical conclusion regarding treatment efficacy. Verhaak demonstrated that while the molecular origins of the tumors vary wildly, the overall prognosis remains devastating. This result is similar to the present project's clinical phenotype data comparison step, which revealed an almost identical median overall survival (357 days vs. 360 days) between two highly distinct molecular cohorts. Both studies ultimately conclude that Glioblastoma's molecular heterogeneity mandates the development of subtype-specific therapies rather than generalized treatment protocols.

# Final Conclusions
## Limitations of the Study
While the pipeline successfully identified distinct transcriptomic profiles, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, dropping the rare outlier clusters (N=5 and N=1) was a statistical necessity to preserve the power of the Mann-Whitney test, but it means the rarest Glioblastoma phenotypes were excluded from biomarker discovery. Second, the use of bulk RNA-seq data measures the average expression of millions of cells. Tumors are highly heterogeneous, meaning healthy tissue or immune cell infiltration within the bulk sample could dilute the specific signatures of the cancer cells.

## Future Improvements and Extensions
Future iterations of this pipeline could incorporate diverse gene expression sequence normalized data to give insight into GBM tumor heterogeneity. Additionally, performing Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) would elevate the current findings by mapping the individual biomarkers (like TFAM and KEAP1) into broader biochemical pathways. Finally, integrating multi-omics data—such as DNA methylation and somatic mutation data—alongside the RNA-seq clusters would provide a wider view of the mechanisms driving these subtypes, such as was done in the study previously discussed by Verhaak et al.

## Learnings Regarding Data Structure
This analysis provided profound insights into the behavior of high-dimensional biological data. The presence of massive class imbalance (121 patients in Subtype 0 vs. 1 in Subtype 3) initially appeared as an algorithmic flaw but was ultimately recognized as a reflection of true biological reality and the limitations of the low sample size of the dataset; tumor subtypes do not exist in equal proportions. Furthermore, the dimensionality reduction phase demonstrated that the vast majority of gene expression variance is driven by a small subset of genes, while the differential expression phase revealed the challenge of "unknown genomic data"—highly active sequence fragments that remain unannotated or deprecated in modern databases, such as was seen in the comparison of the present dataset with the `mygene dataset`.

## Real-World Clinical Connection
Ultimately, the objective of bioinformatic data science is to improve patient outcomes. The discovery that Subtype 0 operates as a hyperactive state (driven by TFAM and KEAP1 gene expressions) while Subtype 1 remains repressed in samples proves that Glioblastoma is functionally multiple distinct diseases. However, the survival analysis revealed a median overall survival of ~360 days for both cohorts. This parity is the most critical real-world takeaway: generalized, standard-of-care treatments for Glioblastoma are currently failing across all patients with inherently different Glioblastoma subtypes. The unique biomarkers isolated in this study highlight the urgent clinical necessity to abandon broad-spectrum chemotherapies and steer toward targeted, personalized metabolic therapy.
